This week we entered the Build: Sprint 1 phase. Unlike Week 7, which focused more on concept and functional requirements, the emphasis this week shifted toward how to translate those requirements into actionable interface decisions. The lecture mentioned that the key theme for Week 8 is modularity—breaking the system into independent, modifiable, reusable, and combinable parts rather than starting from scratch with "page-wide" designs. It also noted that recurring elements like cards, list items, navigation, footers, and layout structures should be treated as reusable components. Meanwhile, the layout should serve as a consistent skeleton across pages, with dynamic content inserted accordingly.

This point is highly relevant to our current project, VintaAchieve. Although our website is centered around an exhibition-led experience, it still includes marketplace, profile, and potentially future community interactions. Without modular thinking, the visual and code consistency would quickly deteriorate as the number of pages increases. Therefore, this week, while finalizing the wireframes, we began treating some design decisions as part of a modular system rather than mere decorative elements on individual pages. For instance, after considering the vintage theme, we confirmed a visual direction primarily based on a brown color palette. This is not merely an aesthetic preference but a design system-level decision: colors, spacing, typography, and recurring components should establish shared rules to ensure exhibitions, marketplaces, and profiles remain stylistically compatible rather than appearing as several separate sub-websites. The lecture also specifically emphasized that the role of design systems is to provide shared rules for components, maintaining consistency in style, spacing, color, typography, and behavior.

Another specific progress this week was completing the first version of the footer code. At first glance, the footer is just a section at the bottom of the page, but from a modular perspective, it is a classic reusable component. Since the footer appears across multiple pages, writing it as a stable and reusable block as early as possible makes it easier to maintain when repeated in pages like marketplace, profile, or exhibition. This also aligns with the lecture's principles of repairability and collaboration. Modularity not only allows a part to be modified independently without disrupting the whole but also facilitates team collaboration, as members can take charge of different sections and then combine them within a unified structure.

Regarding team division of labor, I will primarily be responsible for building the code for the profile and marketplace modules. This division has further made me appreciate the necessity of modular architecture. The lecture's introduction to MVC and code organization reminded me that future page development should not simply involve endlessly copying HTML but should gradually consider which parts belong to the view, which data logic may eventually fall under the model, and which request handling will enter the controller. Particularly, the marketplace will involve repeated item cards, filters, and listing displays, while the profile will also include repeated history or saved-item blocks. Therefore, these pages naturally lend themselves to being built using reusable components and partial views rather than being hard-coded all at once.

Next week, I plan to further investigate whether the masonry/waterfall style layout is suitable for VintaAchieve's marketplace or exhibition browsing experience. This direction has potential because vintage objects are often not uniform in terms of image size, composition, and information density, and waterfall flow may better preserve the feeling of "curved browsing" than rigid grid. But it also brings trade-offs: on the one hand, it may enhance exploration and visual richness, and on the other hand, it may affect scanning efficiency, alignment consistency, and even increase the complexity of responsive implementation. Therefore, the next step I need to evaluate is not just whether it can be made, but whether it truly conforms to our current exhibition led concept and whether it is worth putting into the prototype scope within a limited time. This judgment also responds to the assignment's requirement for blogs: development choices should be aligned with functional requirements, and there should be clear explanations for feasibility, constraints, and trade-offs.
